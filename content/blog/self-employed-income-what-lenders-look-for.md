I've had a number of self-employed clients come to me recently who are concerned about how lenders might view their income if they go to make an application. Whether you're looking to purchase a new property or remortgage your existing one, lenders are going to be looking at broadly the same things.

## Are You Trading Again?

First and foremost, lenders want to know that your business is active and generating income. It's more than likely they'll want to have a really good close look at your **latest few months of business bank statements** to see what's coming in and going out.

This isn't about catching you out. It's about building a picture of your current trading position.

## How Has Your Income Been Affected?

You'll need to confirm how your business and personal income have been affected by any disruption, and whether you needed to draw upon any government support schemes.

If you did access support, then depending on when this was, some lenders may not be willing to lend you the amount you actually require. This isn't a blanket rule, though. Different lenders take different views, which is exactly where broker advice makes a difference.

## What Documents Do Lenders Need?

As ever, your **recent years' personal income documents** will be necessary. That means:

- **SA302 tax calculations** and **tax year overviews** (typically the last two to three years)
- **Business bank statements** (recent months)

If you're a **limited company director**, then your **recent years' company accounts** will be needed too.

## Be Ready for Projections

Don't be surprised if lenders want to see some **projections** as well, meaning a forward-looking view of how your business is expected to perform. This is where your accountant becomes important.

It's a good idea to make sure your mortgage broker has **contact details for your accountant** as early in the process as possible. We may need to speak with them to get the supporting documentation lenders require, and having that line of communication open from day one saves time.

## The Takeaway

Being self-employed doesn't mean getting a mortgage is difficult. It just means the process is a bit more involved. The key is preparation: get your documents in order, be upfront about your trading history, and work with a broker who knows which lenders are most receptive to self-employed applicants.
