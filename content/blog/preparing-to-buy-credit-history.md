Welcome to day two of my five-day guide to preparing to buy a new home. Yesterday we talked about [knowing your deposit](/blog/preparing-to-buy-know-your-deposit). Today we're talking about something equally important: your credit history.

I request an up-to-date credit report from every single one of my clients. It is essential that your mortgage adviser receives your credit report and knows exactly what's in your credit file **before** approaching lenders.

## Why It Matters So Much

Lenders take different approaches to what they find on your credit file. Some look at the detail more carefully than others, and what's acceptable to one lender might be a deal-breaker for another.

When it comes to missed payments, for example, lenders might look at:

- **When** the most recent missed payment was
- **How long** it took you to make the original payment
- **The value** of the missed payment
- **How many** arrears you've accumulated over a period of time

More serious issues like **CCJs, defaults, bankruptcies, and their discharge dates** can also appear on your credit file. These are all critical factors in deciding which lender to submit an application to, and getting the lender choice wrong can mean a declined application that then makes future applications harder.

## Two More Good Reasons

Beyond missed payments, there are other important reasons for checking your credit report:

**All your credit balances in one place.** You might have forgotten about a store card, an old overdraft, or a phone contract that's still technically open. These all count towards your affordability assessment, and knowing about them upfront means no surprises during the application.

**Fraud detection.** Most importantly, you could be in a position to identify any credit that's been taken out fraudulently under your name. You absolutely do not want to be submitting a mortgage application only to find out there's been fraudulent activity on your account. Better to discover it now and resolve it first.

## What to Do

Getting your credit report is quick and free through services like Experian, Equifax, or ClearScore. Download it, have a look through, and bring it to your first appointment. If anything looks unfamiliar or concerning, we can discuss it before it becomes a problem.
