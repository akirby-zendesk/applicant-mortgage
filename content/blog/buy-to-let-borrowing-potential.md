If you're thinking about getting a buy-to-let mortgage, whether it's a property you've previously lived in or something that's always been an investment, you're probably going to want to know how much you can actually borrow.

The way lenders assess buy-to-let mortgages is different to residential property, and it comes down to a combination of factors that might surprise you.

## The Key Factors Lenders Consider

### 1. Rental Value

First and foremost: how much will the property realistically rent for? Lenders use this figure heavily in their calculations. They typically want the rental income to cover 125-145% of the mortgage payment, depending on the lender and your tax bracket. Getting an accurate rental valuation is essential.

### 2. Property Valuation

What would the property sell for on the open market? This determines your loan-to-value ratio, which in turn affects the rates available to you and the maximum you can borrow.

### 3. Your Personal Income

Different lenders will lend you different amounts depending on what your personal income is and what tax bracket you fall under. A higher-rate taxpayer often faces stricter rental coverage requirements than a basic-rate taxpayer, because more of the rental income goes to tax.

### 4. Your Existing Property Portfolio

Lenders will want to know about how many other properties you own, and what your income and outgoings are associated with those properties. If you own four or more mortgaged buy-to-lets, you're classified as a "portfolio landlord" under PRA rules, which means more detailed affordability assessments.

### 5. Your Experience as a Landlord

Finally, lenders want to know about your experience. Are you a seasoned investor or a first-time landlord? Do you own your own residential home?

If you fall into the category of first-time landlord and non-home-owner occupier, there are still lenders out there who will consider you for a buy-to-let mortgage, but the options are more limited, which is exactly where whole-of-market advice makes a difference.

## Why This Matters

Because every lender weighs these factors differently, the amount you can borrow can vary enormously depending on who you apply to. A lender who focuses heavily on rental coverage might offer you more than one who puts more weight on personal income, or vice versa.
