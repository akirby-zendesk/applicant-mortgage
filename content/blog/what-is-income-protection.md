Think about this: your car is fully insured. If you had an accident today, there'd be cover in place to make sure it could be repaired and back on the road in no time.

But what cover would be in place for *you* if, following that accident, you were unable to work for a period of time?

## The Gap Most People Don't Think About

If you don't have any sick pay entitlements, or they're limited to just a few weeks, and you don't have sufficient savings to get by, then you'd need to receive some kind of income to help with your finances while you recover.

That's exactly what income protection is designed for.

## How Income Protection Works

An income protection policy will pay a percentage of your income, whether you're employed or self-employed, for an agreed period of time if you're unable to work due to illness or injury.

The key features:

- **It covers your income**, not a lump sum. You receive regular monthly payments, just like a salary.
- **It's tailored to you.** The policy is set up based on your specific income, your occupation, and how long you'd want the cover to last.
- **It can cover most reasons you'd be unable to work**, including physical illness, injury, and in many cases mental health conditions.
- **It pays out until you're able to return to work**, or until the end of the agreed term, whichever comes first.

## Why It Matters

Most people insure their possessions without thinking twice. But your ability to earn an income is almost certainly the most valuable thing you have. Without it, mortgage payments, bills, and day-to-day living costs don't stop.

Income protection bridges that gap. It means that if something unexpected happens, you're not relying on savings that might not stretch far enough, or on statutory sick pay that may not come close to covering your outgoings.

## Is It Right for You?

Income protection can be tailored to fit different budgets and circumstances. The cost depends on factors like your age, occupation, health, and how long you'd want to wait before the policy starts paying out (the "deferred period").
