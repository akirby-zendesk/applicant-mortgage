If the time has come for you to remortgage or buy a new home, you might be asking yourself: do I go for a fixed rate mortgage or a variable rate mortgage?

It's one of the most common questions I get asked, and the answer depends on your circumstances, your appetite for risk, and how you feel about your monthly budget. But first, you need to understand the difference between the two.

## Fixed Rate Mortgages

This one's fairly self-explanatory. You'll be tied into a mortgage deal for a set period, typically two, three, or five years. During this "initial period," your rate stays the same, meaning your mortgage payment remains exactly the same month on month.

No surprises. No fluctuations. You know exactly what you're paying from day one to the end of the deal.

## Variable Rate Mortgages

Here's where it gets a bit more nuanced, because there's more than one type of variable rate mortgage.

### Standard Variable Rate (SVR)

This is the lender's own rate. If they decide to increase or decrease it, your mortgage payment moves with it. Most borrowers end up on the SVR when their initial deal expires, and it's almost always more expensive than what you were paying before.

### Discounted Rate

This is linked to the lender's SVR, but your personal rate sits at a set percentage below it throughout the initial period. So if the SVR is 5% and your discount is 1%, you'd pay 4%. But if the SVR goes up, your rate goes up too, just with the discount still applied.

### Tracker Rate

This is slightly different. A tracker rate is linked to the Bank of England base rate rather than the lender's own SVR. For example, if your deal is set at 1% above the base rate, you'll know exactly what your payment is to begin with, but if the base rate increases or decreases, your monthly payment moves in line with it.

## So Which Is Right for You?

With variable rate mortgages, there's every possibility you could end up paying less than if you'd gone for a fixed rate. However, you have to weigh that up against the fact that rates could also increase, potentially to something that puts pressure on your budget.

You need to have a really good think about what your monthly outgoings look like and how far you're prepared to stretch if your variable rate were to rise.

If you're uncomfortable with the thought of your monthly mortgage payment changing throughout the initial period, then it could be that a fixed rate is better suited to you. If you've got more flexibility in your budget and want to take advantage of potentially lower rates, a variable option might work.
